Overview

As the world becomes increasingly connected, more people are maintaining and creating relationships, pursuing education, and conducting business across international borders. Despite this global connectivity, one challenge persists: coordinating across time zones remains surprisingly difficult.

This case study displays the communication barriers faced by international users, students, remote professionals, and global teams, who rely on everyday messaging platforms to stay connected and organized. While tools like Slack, WhatsApp, Discord, and Teams have transformed how people communicate, they fall short when it comes to scheduling across time zones. Users are left to manually calculate time differences, switch between apps, and hope nothing gets lost in translation — a process that is error-prone and mentally taxing.

Our team set out to understand this pain point at a deeper level by speaking directly with students who navigate these challenges daily. Through user interviews and research, we sought to uncover how timezone friction affects real communication habits and what an ideal solution might look like. The result is a proposed extension or integration for existing communication platforms that surfaces timezone information naturally within the conversation flow — reducing cognitive load and making cross-timezone scheduling feel effortless.

The opportunity extends beyond convenience. With distributed workforces and international academic programs on the rise, a smarter timezone experience addresses a genuine gap in the market — one that affects millions of users and has yet to be meaningfully solved by any major platform.

User Research

We interviewed three individuals, Marlie (21) from the UK/Netherlands, Eurphie from China, and Takato (20) from Japan, who frequently use communication apps to socialize with friends and family from different time zones. Our interview protocols involved a remote one-hour and two in-person interviews conducted by Shogo and Sasha. We asked for their consent and provided context for the goal that we are aiming for. We asked questions about their navigation progress and obstacles/annoyances that they face. We then followed up with questions about specific aspects that caused some confusion. If we cannot view what they are describing or explaining their process step-by-step, then we asked how it felt, what could be improved upon with the messaging feature, and how it can be improved. We also asked specific questions about certain applications to further understand what seems to work and what does not. We then conclude the interview by asking them if it is possible to schedule another call in the future. The interviews were around an hour each, one being online and the other two being done in person.
